The Capitulation Problem: A Conditional Optimization Framework
sole author
2026 · Independent Research · IIT Roorkee · 484 trials

Behavioral experiment using Gemma 4B (Ollama) as a controlled proxy — 5 domains × 5 pressure variants. LLM-as-Judge methodology with automated capitulation classification. Validated against blind human coding on a stratified sample. Proposed 3-question diagnostic framework.

Key finding: 42.8% combined capitulation — within the 39–52% human-predicted range. P4 anomaly: direct challenge produced 64.6% hard reversals vs expert authority 0% — the Compliance Paradox.

Dark Patterns Spotted

Deliberate design decisions made against users to protect business metrics. All examples from Indian apps — most from personal experience.

Roach Motel
Easy in. Impossible out.

Signup takes 30 seconds. Cancelling takes 7 screens, a call, and a waiting period. The friction is intentionally asymmetric — exactly what I experienced with Paytm's refund flow.

spotted in: Paytm refunds · Indian telecom portals · OTT cancellations
Unverifiable Refund
₹1,600 displayed. ₹600 processed.

MakeMyTrip showed a refund amount at the start of the cancellation flow. The actual amount processed was ₹1,000 less with no itemised breakdown. You only discover the gap after completing the cancellation — too late to back out.

spotted in: MakeMyTrip cancellation · 4-step IVR designed for drop-off · AI agent deployed as human
Hidden Costs
The price that only appears at the last step.

Four screens of checkout. Everything looks fine. Final screen: platform fee ₹5, convenience charge ₹12, GST ₹18. By step 4 you're already committed — and they know it.

spotted in: BookMyShow · MakeMyTrip · every Indian ticketing platform
Fake Scarcity
Countdown timers that reset. "Only 2 left" that never runs out.

Refresh after 10 minutes — still 3 rooms, timer reset. Manufactured scarcity to trigger loss aversion. You're not going to miss out. They just need you to feel like you might.

spotted in: MakeMyTrip · Goibibo · Flipkart sale pages
Confirmshaming
Making the "no" feel like shame.

One button: "Yes, continue." The other: "No thanks, I don't want to save money." The choice is real. The framing is manipulation.

spotted in: subscription apps · food delivery premium plans · e-commerce flash sales
Not all friction is a dark pattern. Paytm's cancellation flow is one — designed to make you give up. Zomato's cancellation warning is borderline — it informs you of a real consequence. The difference is intent: does the friction protect the user or exploit them?
